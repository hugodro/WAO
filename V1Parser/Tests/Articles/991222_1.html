Intel goes wireless
Have your say in the Community

Wednesday, 22nd December

Intel has created a new division to tackle wireless developments. That gives us two powerful messages. One is the continued diversification of Intel away from its "Wintel" history. The other is a further endorsement of the dramatic potential of the wireless market.

Intel wants to sell PC processors. It's a good business, even with competition from AMD and others. In fact, Intel sells much more than processors. It sells a variety of supporting chips, and many PC companies buy whole motherboards from Intel. But the company is anxious not to be seen as merely providing the hardware platform for Windows.

Linux has helped. Increasing numbers of Intel processors now finish up running Linux, simply because of the growing popularity of that combination. But there are two other areas that are crucial for Intel's diversification. One is the 64-bit processor market, where proprietary Unix systems are well ahead of Windows at present. The other is the seething market for new devices, one significant sector of which is wireless.

The market for mobile wireless devices is growing, and much faster than the PC market. Intel has already staked a claim with its 1.6 billion-dollar purchase of DSP Communications, a supplier of chipsets, chip designs, and software for digital cellular products. Intel has also been working hard to get its processors into devices such as PDAs.

Of course, wireless turns the conventional belief that the US is ahead of Europe on its head. Standardisation on GSM across the whole of Europe has helped both equipment makers such as Nokia and carriers such as Vodaphone to world prominence. The US is hampered by a lack of standardisation and patchy coverage.

Given Europe's wireless advantages, and the new technologies that are already in the pipeline, we expect mobile devices to become highly influential in shaping the future of the Internet. Transmission speeds are moving rapidly up, and the mobile carriers are more likely to use aggressive pricing to gain business than fixed line providers such as BT. While broadband based on DSL technology looks to be dominating US access, Europe may well become primarily wireless in its Internet access.

We already know that mobile phones sell to consumers in vast numbers. With a slew of new digital services added, we expect to see yet further rapid growth in the market. Intel is keen to be there.
