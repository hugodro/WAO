    • Japan catches telecom merger fever
    By Bloomberg News
    Special to CNET News.com
    December 16, 1999, 10:30 a.m. PT

    TOKYO--The rampant consolidation in the international telecommunications market finally hits Japan, as the nation's second-largest long-distance carrier buys two firms for more than 2.7 trillion yen ($26 billion).

    DDI said it will buy companies KDD and IDO for more than 2.7 trillion yen ($26 billion) in stock and assumed debt in the nation's biggest takeover. The combined company will be Japan's biggest provider of international services and will have a nationwide wireless network with almost 13 million customers, challenging the dominance of Nippon Telegraph and Telephone (NTT).

    Global telecommunications is consolidating as carriers build or acquire networks to gain customers and compete. The breakup of AT&T in 1984 sparked long-distance competition with MCI Communications and Sprint, while recent laws have encouraged local competition against the Baby Bells.

    More recently, eased European rules spurred new competitors such as Germany's Mannesmann and France's Vivendi to take on former monopolies Deutsche Telekom and France Telecom.

    International companies, too, are seeking opportunities in Japan's 14 trillion yen ($140 billion) market.

    In April, AT&T and British Telecommunications bought 30 percent of Japan Telecom, the third-largest phone company. Cable & Wireless bought International Digital Communications, Japan's second-largest international carrier, and is trying to buy a stake in Tokyo Telecommunications Network.

    Kyocera, a Kyoto, Japan-based maker of cellular phone handsets, and Toyota, the nation's largest automaker, will be the two largest shareholders of the new company.

    For DDI, which lacks a nationwide cellular network, the merger brings a customer base in Tokyo and Nagoya, and KDD's established international network. KDD had already said it would ally with IDO to develop a new generation of mobile phones with high-speed Internet connections and video capability. IDO and DDI have agreed to develop jointly a new generation of phones.

    The new DDI will need the combined resources if it is to compete effectively against NTT and its affiliate, NTT Mobile Communications Network, the world's largest cellular phone company by market capitalization.

    NTT, which was split by the government into three carriers under a holding company in July, is still the dominant phone company in the country through local units NTT East, NTT West and NTT Communications, its international arm.
