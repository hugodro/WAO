By Kathleen Cholewka

The telecommunications industry is particularly susceptible to the laws of Darwinism, especially when it comes to some of the newer entrants in this business. In order to survive, service providers need to move and change with the momentum of the market.

This is true for application service providers (ASPs), as they continue to morph their business models. In order to be successful, analysts say, ASPs need to continue to partner with network infrastructure companies, cater to niche markets and diversify their product lines.

Currently, the market is fragmented with more than 150 ASPs in the U.S. Forrester Research projects that the global market for applications management will reach $25 billion by 2001. This includes high-end apps such as enterprise resource planning (ERP) and desktop applications like E-mail.

Analysts are also predicting the ASP market will undergo rapid consolidation in the next few years as telecom carriers begin to acquire ASPS in orer to sell enhanced services and attract vertical markets.

"[ASPs] are doing fine now, but I really don't see that there is any way for them to scale up and serve millions of customers in the future."

"I wouldn't be shocked if major telecom providers in 2001 start swallowing up ASPs, as they need to reach smaller businesses," says Kneko Burney, senior analyst of markets and opportunities at Cahners In-stat Group.

As the market for hosted applications grows, ASPs will need to focus on what they're good at, hosting apps and selling customer service while hooking up with the right infrastructure and application partners.

ASPs own some of their own network equipment, such as data centers and server farms. However, as their customer demand grows, ASPs will need to look elsewhere for infrastructure and network capacity to be able to handle the load.

"They're doing fine now, but I really don't see that there is any way for them to scale up and serve millions of customers in the future," says Burney.

Evidence of this trend can be seen in a series of symbiotic relationships that have recently taken place. For example, Qwest Communications International (nasdaq: QWST), a fiber network provider, and KPMG, a professional services firm, earlier this year formed a joint venture called Qwest Cyber Solutions to provide Internet-based application hosting services. The venture has developed partnerships with Cisco (nasdaq: CSCO), Hewlett-Packard (nyse: HWP), Microsoft (nasdaq: MSFT), PeopleSoft (nasdaq: PSFT), Oracle (nasdaq: ORCL), SAP (nyse: SAP) and Siebel Systems (nasdaq: SEBL) and hosts applications via eight U.S. data centers.

Of course, infrastructure players will also benefit from such partnerships. Since traditional telecommunications services are well on the way to becoming commodities, carriers are looking to beef up their revenue by selling services beyond simple transport. Once they have secured the necessary network capacity, another key issue that ASPs have to address is an ever-widening range of customers. Analysts say that serving such a diverse market will require ASPs to focus on vertical markets.

"Being able to interface with their customers in a mass-customizable way is crucial," says Charles Gerlach, director of technology research at Mainspring Communications, a strategy consulting firm focusing on e-commerce. "If they are a pure play ASP or a telco trying to be an ASP, they're realizing that they have to develop a vertical industry focus, because there is too much difference across the industry."
