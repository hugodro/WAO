    21-year-old nets $53 million for tech startup

    Copyright © 1999 Nando Media
    Copyright © 1999 Associated Press

    By MARTHA MENDOZA

    PALO ALTO, Calif. (December 16, 1999 8:33 a.m. EST http://www.nandotimes.com) - Angus Davis told his folks not to worry when he was ejected from a prestigious Massachusetts prep school for hacking into the telephone system.

    But even Davis couldn't have predicted that in less than five years he would leverage his scofflaw talents to secure $53 million in funding for a company trying to combine the power of the World Wide Web with the convenience of the telephone.

    On Wednesday, Davis - who at 21 is barely old enough to pop a bottle of champagne - announced that he and his partners at their telephone information company, Tellme Networks Inc., have received $47 million in funding from venture capital firms Benchmark Capital and Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers. The new round of investment brings the company's total funding to $53 million.

    To be sure, Davis isn't the only young person in the Silicon Valley receiving venture capital these days. In the three months ended September, venture capitalists poured $3.36 billion into San Francisco Bay area companies, $2 billion of that going to Internet companies, according to a Money Tree survey.

    But colleagues say Davis is particularly young. In addition, he and his partners are managing to bring to their business a series of unusual alliances - investors from companies like the former Netscape Communications Corp. and bitter rival Microsoft Corp.

    "We sought out that funding on purpose," Davis said. "It would be easy for us to get money. But we wanted those particular investors behind us because they know what they're doing."

    At the heart of his team, which has grown to 51 employees this month, is Davis, an unusually intelligent, rosy-cheeked young man who chose Netscape over college four years ago and has been riding the Internet wave ever since.

    At Netscape, one of his primary duties was working with Justice Department attorneys suing Microsoft. He also helped develop the Netcenter Web portal which would become the company's hallmark product.

    When Netscape was bought by America Online Inc. last year, Davis struck out on his own again.

    By then, he had more than a decade of high-tech experience under his belt. He began programming at 8, worked for an Internet service provider in high school and created Web pages as a hobby as a teen.

    Then there were his problems at Phillips Academy, the tony Andover, Mass., prep school from which his father and grandfather had graduated before heading to Yale University and Harvard Law School.

    Davis, an only child, ended that family tradition when he got caught hacking into the school's telephone system so he could make calls without paying for them.

    Phillips Academy communications director Sharon Britton confirmed that Davis left the school prematurely in 1995, but would not say why.

    Four years later, his telephone techniques are coming in handy.

    Joining with former Netscape vice president of technology Mike McCue, who chose IBM over college 14 years earlier, Davis founded Tellme Networks to bring the Internet revolution to the telephone.

    McCue said Davis' youth is a distinct advantage to their company.

    "He doesn't know necessarily what can't be done," said McCue. "He's incredibly positive and convinces all of us to go for things we might otherwise try to avoid."

    Their seed money - $6 million - came from the unusual alliance of James Barksdale, Netscape's former CEO, and his business rival Brad Silverberg, who used to lead Microsoft Corp.'s Internet strategy.

    Barksdale said that when Netscape originally hired Davis, he was dubious about his youth.

    "But he's one of the brightest, most clever people you could run into," he said.

    Barksdale pointed out that Microsoft CEO Bill Gates, Apple Computer Corp. interim CEO Steve Jobs and Oracle Corp. CEO Larry Ellison are just a few of the high-tech leaders who never graduated from college.

    Barksdale said he has total faith in Davis and his colleagues at Tellme, even though the company has been somewhat vague about what it's going to sell.

    According to a statement released Wednesday, Tellme's new technology will allow consumers to speak simple voice commands into telephones and gain access to a variety of different consumer services. These could include obtaining stock quotes and driving directions or even ordering items.

    Internet analyst Seamus McAteer, director of Web technology strategies at Jupiter Communications, said the Tellme team has "an ambitious agenda."

    "They're trying to succeed where others have failed in the past," he said. "But if they succeed technically, what they do will be very difficult to replicate."
