continued from "ASPs fight for survival"

Some ASPs are concentrating on high-end applications, serving banks and financial companies. Although the banking industry seems most open to outsourcing its accounting applications, selling strictly high-end applications, like SAP and ERP, is not going to make good business sense in the long run, says Lisa Pierce, director of telecommunications at Giga Information Group. ASPs should therefore diversify their product lines. "Nobody's going to be able to grow revenues by just focusing on the tip of the spear," she says. "The next wave of ASPs will have to broaden their offerings."

Hosted apps are in high demand in the small- and medium-sized business market. According to Cahners In-Stat Group, by 2003, small businesses are expected to provide Internet access to more than 34 million employees in the U.S., making them a "natural market" for ASPs. In fact, companies that primarily target enterprise customers with more than 1,000 customers could lose momentum in gaining market share.

"You'll see more mass market offers and packages for specific types of users," Pierce says. "We'll see within the next six months, well-targeted strategies for small businesses."

One ASP that primarily focuses on small businesses is privately held CenterBeam, an all-outsourced Internet protocol (IP) service provider. Earlier this week, CenterBeam announced with Microsoft that it would buy used PCs from small businesses in exchange for their subscriptions to its services, which include new PCs loaded with Microsoft's Office 2000. Centerbeam's competitive advantage is that it supplies all the equipment from a wireless local area network (LAN) to a new computer and manages every bit of the network.

In addition, Qwest, one of the most active players in the ASP market, partnered with Siebel Systems to offer front office software and has made a point of targeting small- to medium-sized businesses. Qwest distributes Siebel's Sales for Workgroups software and hosts other Siebel applications. Another ASP, Eality.com, sells 40 different applications for small-business customers, including timesheets and purchase orders.

While the most important benefits of hosted apps to small businesses are cost reduction and the ability to get to market quickly, that will shift in coming years. Experts say ASPs will increasingly give their customers the ability to rapidly customize those applications.

"Standalone apps are static, but the best-in-class ASPs are going to learn to evolve their apps in response to customer usage," says Gerlach. ASPs, for example, will eventually be able to tune an e-commerce application according to customer use of a web site, Gerlach predicts.
